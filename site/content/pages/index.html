The Exhibit Cost Record

The public record of what museum exhibits, interactives, and AV actually cost.

The record of what exhibits and interactive media cost already exists in public — federal contracts, grants, state procurement portals, UK tenders, and nonprofit tax filings. It has just never been assembled in one place. No firm's internal finances appear here; every row traces to a source you can check yourself.

Five public sources, one comparable record

Every transaction type discloses cost differently. This dataset normalizes them into one schema — recipient, client, year, amount, description, and a consistent project-scope classification — while keeping each row's own source distinct and citable.

Federal contractsUSAspending.gov awards for exhibit design, fabrication, AV integration, and interpretive media — Smithsonian, NPS, and other federal agencies, 2015–present.
Federal grantsNSF's Advancing Informal STEM Learning program and IMLS museum-grant programs — the science-center and small-museum slice federal contracts miss.
State contractsOpen-checkbook and contract-transparency portals in Texas, California, New York, and Washington.
UK tendersPublic tenders from UK national museums, kept as non-US comparables and never mixed into US figures.
IRS Form 990 disclosuresEvery nonprofit museum must disclose its five highest-paid independent contractors over $100K — the only source that captures nonprofit museum spending directly.

The scope-normalization standard

Published exhibit-cost figures vary mostly by what's bundled into the number — not by the underlying market. The canonical unit of comparison across every row is the full interactive scope, hardware and software combined — design, software, content, hardware, and integration — excluding exhibit fabrication and base-building infrastructure. Awards that deviate are flagged (fabrication_inclusive) rather than silently mixed in. Read the full standard on the methodology page.

About the data

Every row traces to a public, independently checkable source — a government procurement record, a grant database, or a nonprofit's own IRS filing. This is a descriptive record, not price advice, and makes no commentary on any firm's pricing. Data is licensed CC BY 4.0. See the About page for the full rules of the road.
