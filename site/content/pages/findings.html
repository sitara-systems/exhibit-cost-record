What the data indicates about pricing

Every figure below is computed directly from the dataset and can be reproduced by filtering the browse page. This is a descriptive record, not price advice — each finding carries the caveats that matter for reading it responsibly.

1. A clear scope-driven price ladder

Within federal awards — the dataset's largest and most consistently classified single source — the median obligated amount rises steadily with project scope, from a single touch kiosk all the way to a whole-museum exhibit fit-out.

Kiosk (n=18)$36,629 Single interactive (n=77)$44,919 Maintenance (n=81)$59,825 Multi-station (n=288)$227,879 Building-wide (n=6)$1,091,455

That's roughly a 30× spread in medians across five classes — not a surprising direction, but the first time it's been shown with real award-level numbers rather than asserted.

Federal medians only (grants and state contracts skew the combined multi-station median lower, to $162,463, because nonprofit-grant and state awards tend smaller). The federal building_wide rung was a real gap until a 2026-07-22 follow-up pass found six federal rows sitting misclassified as `multi_station`/`other` — see finding 6. A 2026-07-22 cross-reference against the Experiential Design Index added 144 federal rows across several classes (mostly single_interactive and multi_station National Park Service design-only task orders), which is most of why the single-interactive and multi-station medians and n moved from the prior pass.

2. Fabrication bundling changes the number by roughly 1.53×

This is the dataset's central methodological point, now backed by data instead of assumption. Awards that bundle exhibit fabrication into the scope cost meaningfully more than awards covering design, software, AV, and integration alone — at the same project-class level.

Fabrication included$150,000 median Fabrication excluded$98,040 median

Published exhibit-cost figures that don't specify what's bundled in are close to meaningless for comparison — two "exhibit cost" numbers can differ by 53% purely on scope definition, with no difference in the underlying market. Every row in this dataset carries the fabrication_inclusive flag specifically so this doesn't get averaged away.

Computed across all non-other classes and all USD sources; rows with fabrication_inclusive: unknown are excluded from both sides of the comparison, not counted as either.

3. Nonprofit museums pay their standing vendors a different kind of number

IRS Form 990 contractor disclosures measure something federal and state procurement data can't see: what a nonprofit museum pays one firm across a whole tax year, not per project.

Median annual compensation (n=58)$569,553 Mean$826,091 Largest (AMNH / Design and Production Inc.)$7,143,403

These figures aren't directly comparable to the single-project award amounts elsewhere in the dataset — a museum's annual payment to a standing exhibit vendor can span several concurrent projects. But they're the only window this dataset has into nonprofit museum spending at all, since that spending mostly never touches a government procurement system.

Only 58 of ~537 extracted contractor rows (across 156 researched museums) were kept as plausibly exhibit/AV/design related — see the methodology page for the full keep/skip rule.

4. A small number of firms do most of the government-facing work

Across federal and state awards, five recipients account for a large share of all rows in the dataset:

Capitol Exhibit Services279 rows Color-Ad129 rows Electrosonic76 rows Explus60 rows Southern Custom Exhibits of Alabama55 rows

That's 599 of 1,344 federal-plus-state rows — about 45% — concentrated in five firms. This isn't unusual for government procurement (a handful of firms build the past-performance record needed to keep winning), but it means award-level pricing in this dataset is disproportionately shaped by how these specific firms price, not a broad market sample.

Southern Custom Exhibits of Alabama displaced Ideum from the fifth spot after the 2026-07-22 Experiential Design Index cross-reference surfaced real additional National Park Service awards to the firm the original sweep had missed.

Row count, not dollar share — a firm with many small task orders (like Capitol Exhibit Services' trade-show and small-repair volume) shows up differently than one with fewer, larger awards.

5. One vendor's kiosk product line prices almost like a catalog

12 of the dataset's 31 kiosk-class rows (39%) are a single vendor, Ideum — and their federal multi-touch table awards form a fairly legible price ladder by size and customization: roughly $16,700 for a small table up to $199,000 for their largest custom unit. It's the closest thing in this dataset to visible product-line pricing from a single dominant supplier, rather than a one-off custom bid.

6. Whole-museum exhibit fit-outs still need the headline caveat — but there's real federal data now

Until a 2026-07-22 follow-up pass, this class had zero federal rows at all. A targeted search found six federal awards that had been sitting misclassified as multi_station or other — each describes an unqualified, whole-facility exhibit scope (a brand-new Smithsonian museum's inaugural exhibits, the sole fabrication contract for an entire other national museum, a NASA visitor center's complete exhibit rebuild, three National Park Service visitor-center exhibit contracts). The class is now 13 rows across all sources:

Federal n6, median $1,091,455 All sources n13 Range$80,020 – $57,442,500

Don't cite the all-sources median without reading this: two of the largest values ($57.4M and $13.5M, both a single vendor's Texas Historical Commission awards) are flagged scope_confidence: low in the source data — they read as IDIQ contract-vehicle ceiling values, not confirmed single-project spend. The federal rows found in the follow-up pass are cleaner: two (Smithsonian's NMAAHC inaugural exhibits at $7.07M obligated / $48.6M ceiling, and the National Museum of the U.S. Army's exhibit fabrication at $45.2M obligated / $69.6M ceiling) are medium-confidence with an explicit whole-museum scope; the other four are smaller visitor-center-scale fit-outs ($395,820 – $1.4M).

Sparse by design still applies — the dataset publishes this class with its real, still-small n rather than padding it. Three of the six federal rows carry scope_confidence: low themselves (the title alone doesn't explicitly confirm full-facility scope the way the others do) — see the methodology page for the full reclassification writeup.

7. UK figures land in the same range as mid-size US work

UK museum-sector tenders (non-US comparables, kept in their own file) show gallery-scale fit-outs in the same order of magnitude as US multi_station awards — roughly £380,000 to £900,000. The dataset's single UK building_wide row is a £2.2M design-team appointment (RIBA stages 2–6, two museums' new galleries) that explicitly excludes fabrication — about a third the size of the (caveated) US building-wide median, which is consistent with finding 2's fabrication-bundling effect showing up again in a completely different procurement system.

Currency and market differences mean these are directional comparisons, not apples-to-apples conversions. See methodology.

8. The biggest dollar figures in the dataset are not exhibit costs

The largest raw obligated amounts in the whole dataset — several in the $100M–$545M range — all sit in project_class: other, not any exhibit-specific class. They're whole-museum construction-management and renovation contracts (e.g. the Smithsonian's National Air and Space Museum and National Museum of African American History and Culture revitalizations), correctly excluded from exhibit-specific figures by the scope-normalization standard.

This is worth stating plainly: if you sort this dataset by raw dollar amount without checking project_class, you will conclude exhibits cost far more than they do. The classification system exists specifically to prevent that conflation.
